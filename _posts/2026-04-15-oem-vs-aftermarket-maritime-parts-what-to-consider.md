The OEM-versus-aftermarket question is presented as a binary in catalogues and a continuum in real procurement. The genuine OEM part is the safe choice; an equivalent aftermarket part can be a substantial saving with no operational difference. The mistake costs are not symmetrical, though, and the decision turns on a small number of specific factors rather than a general preference.

## What "OEM" and "aftermarket" actually mean

**OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer):** the part is sourced through the engine or equipment maker's own supply chain, with their part number, packaging, and documentation. For the marine sector this is Wärtsilä, MAN ES, Caterpillar Marine, Alfa Laval, etc.

**Aftermarket:** any part that fits the same application, made by a third-party manufacturer. Quality varies enormously. At the top end, aftermarket suppliers are former OEM contractors producing identical parts under their own brand; at the bottom end, the part is a copy with no controls on materials, tolerances, or testing.

**Genuine OEM-sourced from a third party** is a third category that's often labelled aftermarket in catalogues but is functionally identical to OEM. This happens when a stockist holds OEM-made inventory acquired through legitimate channels.

## When OEM is the only acceptable choice

There are situations where the cost difference is irrelevant because aftermarket is not viable:

- **Class-related components on classed vessels.** Anything that affects vessel classification — main engine critical components, certain pumps, safety-system parts — typically needs a part with traceable certification the class society will accept. Aftermarket parts without this paperwork can mean the part is replaced again at the next survey, at the operator's cost.
- **Warranty period on new equipment.** Fitting an aftermarket part during the warranty window usually voids the warranty for the affected system. The saving on one part rarely justifies the loss of cover on the rest.
- **Common-rail injection systems and other tightly toleranced precision components.** These are rarely worth gambling on. The failure mode of a sub-spec injector is not "slightly worse performance" — it's catastrophic damage to the engine.
- **Class-1 electrical components in safety circuits.** Where the consequence of failure is loss of vessel control or fire suppression, the documentation chain matters more than the cost.

## When aftermarket is reasonable

Plenty of parts are commodities in everything but their part number:

- **Standard fasteners, gaskets, O-rings, and seals** — provided the material specification matches (the right grade of stainless, the right rubber compound). The OEM part is typically a standard item rebranded.
- **Filter elements** — many marine filters are made by Hydac, Parker, Pall, or similar specialists who supply both the OEM channel and aftermarket equivalents. Sourcing direct is cheaper and the part is identical.
- **Bearings** — major bearing brands (SKF, FAG, NSK, Timken) supply OEMs across the industry. Their catalogue numbers are often more useful than the OEM-rebranded equivalents and the parts are interchangeable.
- **Routine consumables** — lube oil filters, fuel filters, coolant, etc. The OEM channel for these adds cost without adding function.

## The documentation trap

Where operators get caught is when an aftermarket part passes physical inspection but fails paperwork inspection at survey or insurance claim. Two specific scenarios:

1. **Insurance claim after engine damage.** The insurer's surveyor identifies a non-OEM critical component fitted during the prior maintenance interval. The claim is reduced or rejected on the basis that aftermarket parts contributed to the failure — even if they didn't.
2. **Class survey on a classed vessel.** A safety-related component without traceable certification fails the survey. The vessel cannot trade until the part is replaced with an acceptable equivalent. The cost of being off-hire dwarfs the original parts saving.

The lesson isn't "always buy OEM." It's that the price difference between an OEM and a high-quality aftermarket part is often less than the cost of one bad outcome — and the bad outcome is more likely if the part is in a regulated category.

## A practical decision framework

For each part, ask in this order:

1. **Is it on a classed component on a classed vessel?** If yes, OEM or class-approved aftermarket only.
2. **Is the equipment under manufacturer warranty?** If yes, OEM only.
3. **Is the part safety-critical or in a precision system?** If yes, OEM unless you have direct experience with a specific aftermarket source.
4. **Is the part a commodity (fastener, filter, bearing, seal)?** Aftermarket from a known specialist is usually equivalent and cheaper.
5. **Is the OEM part still available?** If the OEM has discontinued production, aftermarket or compatible-replacement sourcing is the only route. (See: obsolete parts.)

## When the OEM part is not available at all

Older vessels eventually run into discontinued OEM parts. The route here is to identify a compatible replacement — the part doesn't need to be identical, it needs to fit the application and meet the operating spec. This is where a sourcing partner with engineering literacy is worth more than a catalogue: the right replacement is often hidden in another OEM's range or in a specialist aftermarket supplier's catalogue, but it requires comparing specifications rather than searching by part number.

We do this regularly — most operators with a fleet of any age will at some point face a part that has been OEM-discontinued for a decade. It's solvable; it just costs a different kind of effort than ordering a current part.

## The summary

Don't decide OEM-vs-aftermarket on price alone. Decide it on the **regulatory context** (is this a classed or warranty-protected part?) and the **failure consequences** (what happens if the part is sub-spec?). When both factors are low, aftermarket is the reasonable default. When either is high, OEM is the cheaper choice once you account for the alternative.
