If quotes are slow, the RFQ is usually the reason. Suppliers can't price a part they can't identify, and most of the back-and-forth that delays a maritime parts order is a vendor trying to fill in the blanks of an incomplete request.

This is a practical reference for what a workable RFQ looks like and what each field gives the supplier.

## The five fields that matter

Every parts request, regardless of complexity, should specify:

1. **Part number** — manufacturer's reference, exactly as printed on the part or in the parts catalogue
2. **OEM / brand name** — Wärtsilä, MAN B&W, Caterpillar, Alfa Laval, etc.
3. **Vessel or engine model** — engine type and bore/stroke, or the unit the part fits
4. **Quantity** — including units (sets, pieces, metres of cable, etc.)
5. **Urgency** — needed by date, or "AOG" / vessel off-hire status if applicable

Anything else is useful, but these five let a supplier go directly to a vendor for a price. Skip one and the supplier has to come back to you before they can do anything.

## What each field unlocks

### Part number
The part number is the single most efficient field. Two parts can have similar descriptions and very different prices — a non-return valve from a 2008 Sulzer ZA40S is not interchangeable with one from a 2021 Wärtsilä 32. The part number ends the ambiguity.

If you don't have it, send a clear photograph of the nameplate or the parts manual page. Avoid descriptions like "the small black valve from the cooling system" — these get translated by the vendor into a guess, and the guess shows up in the quote.

### OEM name
Same component, different manufacturers, different stock locations. A Hydac filter and a Parker filter at first glance perform the same function; the supply chain to source each is completely different. Naming the OEM is what tells the supplier whose stock to check.

### Vessel or engine model
This catches a category of mistakes the part number alone won't. The same part number sometimes appears on two engine variants with different specifications. Quoting the engine model lets the supplier verify the part fits before sending the quote — not after the part has shipped.

For non-engine items (pumps, electrical, sensors), the equivalent information is the equipment make and model, plus serial number if available.

### Quantity
Obvious, often skipped. "I need spares for the next docking" is not a quantity. If you genuinely don't know yet — for example you're scoping a refit — say so explicitly: "Provisional quantity 6, to be confirmed against survey." That gives the supplier an indicative price without committing you, and avoids the awkward case of a binding quote you can't act on.

### Urgency
This determines what the quote actually means. A part with a 12-week ex-stock lead time can also be sourced air-freight from a different region in 72 hours — but the price is different and the supplier needs to know which one to quote. "Urgent" is too vague; specify the latest date the part can arrive at port.

If the vessel is off-hire and demurrage is running, say so. It changes the supplier's behaviour from "find a price" to "find the part now, price after."

## Common mistakes that delay quotes

- **One email, six unrelated parts** — combine if they're for the same vessel or system; otherwise split. Mixed lists slow everything down because each part may go to a different vendor.
- **Photos without context** — a clear photo of a nameplate is gold. A photo of the part installed, with no nameplate visible, is a guessing game.
- **"Send me your best price"** without quantity or urgency. The lowest price for one piece in eight weeks is a different number from the lowest price for ten pieces in ten days.
- **Using description-only requests for serialised parts.** Bearings, seals, and gaskets are particularly bad for this — the catalogue entry is often the only way to specify them correctly.
- **Forgetting class requirements.** If a part needs to be classed (DNV, LR, ABS, etc.), say so up front. Sourcing without that constraint and then discovering it later starts the search again.

## A workable RFQ template

> **Vessel:** MV [name], IMO [number]
> **Engine / equipment:** [make + model + serial]
> **Required by:** [date], [delivery port]
> **Off-hire status:** [yes / no]
>
> **Item 1**
> Part no: [number]
> OEM: [brand]
> Description: [short]
> Quantity: [n]
> Class certification required: [yes / no — which society]
>
> **Item 2** [...]

Copy this into an email; spend two minutes filling it in; expect a quote within hours rather than days.

## When you genuinely don't have the information

Sometimes the part number is unreadable, the manual is missing, or the vessel changed hands and the new operator has no records. In that case, send what you have — photos, a sample part if possible, a sketch of the assembly — and flag it as "ID assistance needed." A capable supplier should be able to identify the part for you. Just don't disguise an identification problem as a quote request — they're different jobs and conflating them costs days.

A good RFQ is the cheapest way to compress the time between "we need this part" and "the part is on a ship." The information costs nothing to provide and removes most of the delay before procurement even begins.
