Marine spare parts clear UAE customs reasonably smoothly when the paperwork is right and not at all when it isn't. The procedure is well-defined; most of the friction comes from one of a small number of recurring mistakes. This is the working checklist we use ourselves.

## The required documents

For a standard parts import to a Dubai entity, customs needs:

1. **Commercial invoice** — issued by the seller, on letterhead, addressed to the consignee, with itemised line items and total value
2. **Packing list** — gross/net weights, dimensions, package count, matched to the invoice
3. **Bill of lading** (sea freight) or **air waybill** (air freight) — original or telex-released
4. **Certificate of origin** — usually a chamber-of-commerce stamped document from the origin country
5. **HS code** — Harmonised System code per item, applied at declaration

For some categories, additional documents may be needed:
- **MSDS (material safety data sheets)** for chemical-containing items, batteries, or pressurised components
- **Class society certificate** for safety-related parts on classed vessels (LR, DNV, ABS, BV, etc.)
- **End-use certificate** if the goods are dual-use under any export-control regime

Missing any of the first five usually stops the consignment. Missing the second-tier documents typically causes a temporary hold, resolvable with same-day submission.

## The commercial invoice — what actually matters

The commercial invoice is where most clearance problems originate. Customs reads it as the source of truth for value and item identity, and disagreements between the invoice and the rest of the file cause delays.

A good invoice:
- States the full legal name of the seller and buyer, with addresses
- Carries an invoice number, date, and Incoterms (CIF, FOB, EXW, DAP, etc.)
- Lists each item separately with: description, part number, OEM brand, quantity, unit price, total
- Shows the currency and total amount
- Lists any discounts or adjustments separately rather than netting them silently into a unit price
- States the country of origin per line item

The line-item description matters. "Spare parts" or "machinery components" is not enough — customs uses the description to verify the HS code, and a vague description forces a manual review. "Cylinder head gasket, MAN B&W 6L23/30, P/N 1234" leaves no ambiguity.

## HS codes — a small detail with big consequences

The HS (Harmonised System) code determines the duty rate, the VAT treatment, and whether the item triggers any additional regulatory check. Marine spare parts span many chapters depending on the item: engines and engine parts (Chapter 84), pumps and valves (Chapter 84), electrical equipment (Chapter 85), navigation and measurement instruments (Chapter 90), and so on.

Two practical points:

- **HS classification is the importer's responsibility, not the seller's.** A seller-suggested HS code is not binding on UAE customs. The right code is determined by what the goods actually are, not what label was put on them.
- **Wrong HS codes are a common audit finding.** Customs can re-classify after the fact and charge the difference in duty plus penalties. If you're not sure, ask the customs broker or supplier to verify against the current GCC tariff schedule rather than copy whatever was used last time.

## VAT — UAE buyers vs international buyers

UAE imports are subject to 5% VAT on the customs value (CIF + duty). Where it gets nuanced:

- **UAE-VAT-registered buyers** can recover the import VAT through their VAT return, so net cost effect is zero — but only if the invoice and customs declaration are addressed to the registered entity. Mistakes here cause non-recoverable VAT leakage.
- **Non-GCC buyers** receiving an export from UAE are zero-rated under UAE VAT law, provided the goods leave the country and the export is documented (commercial invoice marked for export, transport documents on file).
- **Reverse-charge mechanism** applies in some cross-border scenarios — usually where the supplier is outside the UAE and the customer is a registered importer. The Dubai customs broker handles the declaration mechanics; the relevant point for the buyer is to make sure their TRN appears on the import documents.

If you are an importer, your **TRN (Tax Registration Number)** must be on the import declaration to be eligible for VAT recovery. We've seen consignments delivered to the right address but declared under the wrong TRN — the recovery is then blocked until the declaration is amended.

## Dubai Customs Code

UAE-based importers need a Dubai Customs Code (also called the importer code) to clear shipments through Dubai ports and airports. Without it, the consignment can land but it cannot be cleared in your name.

Application is straightforward but not instant — typically a few business days through a freight forwarder or directly via the Dubai Trade portal. If you import even occasionally, set this up before your first shipment, not when the shipment is sitting at the port.

The number is also useful as a credential: many international suppliers and forwarders ask for it as evidence that the buyer is a legitimate importer.

## EORI — when it applies

EORI (Economic Operators Registration and Identification) numbers are an EU concept, not a UAE one. The relevance to UAE-based maritime parts work is when goods transit through, originate from, or end up in the EU — at which point an EORI number is required for the EU-side customs declaration.

For Asil Trading we maintain a Netherlands EORI (NL827475391) which covers EU-side movements. If your supplier is in Europe and the goods are routed through Rotterdam, Antwerp, or another EU port for transhipment, having an EORI on file (yours or your forwarder's) avoids holds in transit.

## Common mistakes that cost time

- **Incoterm mismatch.** The invoice says CIF, the actual freight contract says EXW, and the customs value calculation diverges. Customs will pick whichever is higher.
- **Rounded prices.** A unit price of "approximately $500" is not a customs value. Customs needs the actual transaction amount.
- **Used parts declared as new** (or vice versa). Affects HS classification and sometimes triggers regulatory review.
- **Dual-use items not declared.** Some marine items (encryption-capable comms, certain sensors) fall under export controls and need explicit handling. Misclassification can cause a much larger problem than a delayed shipment.
- **Goods consigned to the wrong entity.** A consignee that doesn't match the importer code, the TRN, or the receiving warehouse causes a clearance freeze.

## A working pre-shipment checklist

Before goods leave the seller's premises, confirm:

- Invoice has correct buyer name, address, TRN, and Incoterms
- Each item has full description, part number, OEM, origin
- Invoice value matches the commercial agreement (no last-minute changes)
- Packing list matches invoice (item count, weight)
- HS codes assigned and reviewed
- BL/AWB consigned to the correct UAE entity, with correct contact for the broker
- Certificate of origin attached
- Any class certificate or MSDS attached if applicable

Five extra minutes pre-shipment usually saves days at the port.

## When to use a broker vs handle it in-house

If you import marine parts more than once a quarter, having a customs broker with maritime experience on a retainer is almost always worth it. The fee is small relative to one stuck consignment, and brokers stay current on tariff changes that an internal team won't track.

For one-off imports, the freight forwarder typically arranges clearance as part of the door-to-door service. That works, but the operator should still review the declaration before submission — it's the importer's name on the customs file, not the forwarder's.
